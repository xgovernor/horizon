Welcome to an extended entry in our private knowledge library. In the summer and fall of 1933, amid the Great Depression and the early days of Franklin D. Roosevelt’s New Deal, retired U.S. Marine Corps Major General Smedley Darlington Butler claimed he was approached by representatives of wealthy Wall Street interests and businessmen who sought to recruit him to lead a coup against the newly elected president. The alleged plot involved creating a large veterans’ organization modeled on European fascist movements, raising an army of up to 500,000 men, marching on Washington, D.C., and installing Butler as a dictator or “Secretary of General Affairs” while reducing Roosevelt to a figurehead. The backers reportedly feared Roosevelt’s policies threatened capitalism and their financial interests, including opposition to abandoning the gold standard. Butler, a highly decorated veteran and vocal critic of “war as a racket,” played along to gather details before reporting the scheme. His testimony before the House Special Committee on Un-American Activities (McCormack–Dickstein Committee) in 1934 led to a congressional investigation that found evidence the plot had been discussed and planned, though no one was prosecuted and major newspapers largely dismissed or ridiculed the story. In this detailed document, we examine the historical context, key figures and alleged approaches, Butler’s testimony, the congressional investigation and findings, media reaction, named individuals and their denials, alternative interpretations, and the lasting legacy—all drawn from Butler’s sworn testimony, the McCormack–Dickstein Committee report, contemporary newspaper accounts, and subsequent historical analyses.

## Background and Context

The plot emerged during one of the most turbulent periods in modern U.S. history. The Great Depression had devastated the economy, with massive unemployment and bank failures. Roosevelt’s election in 1932 and the implementation of the New Deal— including banking reforms, public works programs, and the abandonment of the gold standard in 1933—were seen by some wealthy conservatives as dangerous steps toward socialism or dictatorship. Many business leaders admired European fascist models (Mussolini’s Italy in particular) for their anti-communist stance and strongman leadership. The American Legion, a powerful veterans’ organization, was viewed as a potential recruiting ground for a private army. Discontented veterans from the 1932 Bonus Army march on Washington added to the volatile atmosphere. Against this backdrop, certain financiers and industrialists allegedly explored extra-constitutional means to curb Roosevelt’s agenda.


# Key Context (Documented)

- Timeframe: Approaches began ~July–August 1933; Butler’s public revelations November 1934
- Economic backdrop: Great Depression; New Deal policies opposed by many business elites
- Butler’s profile: Twice awarded Medal of Honor; outspoken critic of U.S. military interventions on behalf of business interests (“War Is a Racket,” 1935)
- Alleged model: Fascist organizations in Europe (e.g., Mussolini’s March on Rome)


**Trust note**: The broader economic and political context is undisputed. Specific details of the alleged plot rely heavily on Butler’s testimony and supporting evidence presented to Congress.

## Key Figures and Alleged Approaches

The central figure in Butler’s account was Gerald C. MacGuire, a bond salesman for Grayson M.-P. Murphy & Co. (linked to J.P. Morgan interests) and former commander of the Connecticut American Legion. MacGuire, along with William Doyle (Massachusetts American Legion commander), allegedly first approached Butler in July 1933 at his home in Pennsylvania. They discussed Butler running for American Legion national commander with financial backing.

Subsequent meetings escalated:
- MacGuire reportedly offered Butler $18,000 to deliver a speech advocating return to the gold standard at the Legion convention.
- He described plans for a large veterans’ organization funded by wealthy backers (initially mentioning $3 million, later up to $300 million or more in some accounts).
- The goal: Organize 500,000 veterans to march on Washington, demand Roosevelt appoint a “Secretary of General Affairs” (effectively a dictator), and force him into a ceremonial role due to alleged poor health.
- If successful, Butler would lead the force; alternatives mentioned included other generals.

MacGuire claimed backing from prominent figures including Robert Sterling Clark (Singer Sewing Machine fortune), Grayson Murphy, and others tied to DuPont, Morgan, and Remington interests. He had traveled to Europe to study fascist movements and reportedly showed enthusiasm for Mussolini-style organization.

Butler, suspicious, continued conversations to elicit more details and confided in a reporter (Paul Comly French). He then reported the matter, leading to congressional scrutiny.

**Trust note**: These details come directly from Butler’s sworn testimony before the McCormack–Dickstein Committee. MacGuire denied key elements but admitted some meetings and discussions about veterans’ organizations and gold standard advocacy. No independent corroborating witnesses to all conversations have been documented.

## The McCormack–Dickstein Committee Investigation

In November 1934, the House Special Committee on Un-American Activities (chaired by Rep. John W. McCormack and Rep. Samuel Dickstein) investigated Nazi propaganda and related activities. Butler’s allegations were referred to them. Hearings included testimony from Butler, MacGuire, French, and others.

Butler’s testimony (November 1934) was detailed and consistent. He described multiple meetings, specific offers, and names mentioned by MacGuire.

The committee’s final report (February 15, 1935) stated: “In the last few weeks of the committee’s official life it received evidence showing that certain persons had made an attempt to establish a fascist organization in this country… There is no question that these attempts were discussed, were planned, and might have been placed in execution when and if the financial backers deemed it expedient.”

The report validated Butler’s core claims about the discussions but noted limited hard evidence beyond testimony. It recommended further investigation, but the committee’s mandate ended, and no prosecutions followed. Some testimony portions (including hearsay naming additional backers) were redacted or not fully publicized.

**Trust note**: The committee’s conclusions are primary source material (available via congressional records and Wikisource). The report took Butler’s testimony seriously but found insufficient evidence for criminal charges. Media at the time often portrayed the story as exaggerated or a “hoax.”

## Named Individuals, Denials, and Media Reaction

Alleged backers or associates mentioned in testimony or later analyses included:
- Robert Sterling Clark
- Grayson M.-P. Murphy
- Irénée du Pont
- J.P. Morgan interests (indirectly)
- Members of the American Liberty League (formed 1934 to oppose the New Deal)

All named individuals or their representatives denied involvement in any coup plot. MacGuire admitted meeting Butler but claimed discussions were only about Legion politics and the gold standard. Major newspapers (e.g., *New York Times*) initially dismissed the story as incredible or a “gigantic hoax.” Coverage was mixed, with some outlets downplaying it due to ownership ties to business interests. The story faded quickly from public discourse.

**Trust note**: Denials are consistent across contemporary statements. No smoking-gun documents (contracts, funding records) proving a fully operational coup have surfaced. Historians debate whether the plot was serious planning, loose talk by MacGuire to impress Butler, or a mix.

## Alternative Interpretations and Debates

- **Serious Plot**: Supporters argue the committee’s validation, MacGuire’s European trip to study fascism, financial resources of named backers, and the era’s elite hostility to the New Deal indicate a genuine (if embryonic) threat. Butler’s credibility as a decorated general who had no motive to fabricate is emphasized.
- **Exaggeration or Hoax**: Critics note reliance on one man’s testimony, lack of corroborating physical evidence or additional whistleblowers, and MacGuire’s possible exaggeration or scam. Some view it as political theater or Butler seeking publicity.
- **Partial Reality**: Many modern historians conclude discussions occurred and a loose network of anti-New Deal businessmen explored options, but it never reached operational stages due to Butler’s refusal and lack of unified commitment.

No evidence links the plot directly to a fully coordinated “Wall Street” consensus; it appears more as exploratory talks among a faction of conservatives.

**Trust note**: The committee found discussions and planning credible. Extreme claims (fully ready army, imminent march) lack strong support. Scholarly consensus leans toward a real but limited conspiracy that never advanced far.

## Combining Elements: Elite Discontent and Failed Coup

The Business Plot combines economic crisis, elite opposition to democratic reforms, admiration for European fascism, and reliance on a popular military figure:

> ## Overlapping Factors
>
> - Great Depression and New Deal policies alienating powerful financial interests.
> - Use of veterans as a potential private army, echoing fascist takeovers in Europe.
> - Approach to a decorated, anti-imperialist general (Butler) seen as manipulable or popular.
> - Congressional validation of discussions/planning without leading to prosecutions.
> - Media dismissal or downplaying, limiting public awareness.
> - Butler’s decision to expose rather than join, halting any momentum.

These elements highlight tensions between capitalism and democracy during crisis, and how far some elites considered extra-legal solutions.

## Conclusion

The Business Plot of 1933 remains one of the most intriguing and under-discussed episodes in American political history. Retired Major General Smedley Butler alleged that representatives of wealthy businessmen approached him to lead a fascist-style coup involving a veterans’ army to overthrow or sideline President Franklin D. Roosevelt. The McCormack–Dickstein Committee investigated, heard detailed testimony, and concluded in its 1935 report that such attempts were discussed, planned, and could have been executed under the right conditions. However, no individuals were prosecuted, key named figures denied involvement, and contemporary media largely ridiculed or minimized the story. Whether the plot represented serious intent by a cabal of financiers or inflated talk by intermediaries remains debated, but the congressional findings confirm that anti-New Deal conversations crossed into plotting territory. For personal study, the case illustrates elite resistance to populist reforms, the role of military figures in political intrigue, and the challenges of holding powerful interests accountable. It serves as a reminder that threats to democratic norms can emerge from unexpected quarters during times of economic stress. Primary sources—Butler’s testimony and the committee report—provide the strongest foundation for evaluation.
