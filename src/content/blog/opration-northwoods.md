In March 1962, the U.S. Joint Chiefs of Staff (JCS), chaired by General Lyman L. Lemnitzer, developed a top-secret set of proposals known as Operation Northwoods. The plan outlined a series of false flag operations—covert actions designed to appear as if carried out by the Cuban government under Fidel Castro—to generate public and international outrage sufficient to justify direct U.S. military intervention and overthrow of the Castro regime. Proposed tactics included staging terrorist attacks in U.S. cities, hijacking or destroying aircraft (including a drone airliner made to look like a civilian flight), sinking boats carrying Cuban refugees or a U.S. ship in Guantanamo Bay, assassinating Cuban émigrés, and orchestrating riots or sabotage blamed on Cuba. The memorandum, titled "Justification for U.S. Military Intervention in Cuba," was presented to Secretary of Defense Robert McNamara as part of the broader Operation Mongoose anti-Castro efforts. President John F. Kennedy ultimately rejected the proposals, and they were never implemented. The documents remained classified for decades until declassified in the late 1990s by the Assassination Records Review Board and further publicized in 2001 through the National Security Archive and James Bamford's book *Body of Secrets*. In this detailed document, we examine the historical context, specific proposed operations, key figures, the decision-making process and rejection, declassification and public revelation, official responses, alternative interpretations, and the lasting significance—all drawn directly from the declassified JCS memorandum, contemporaneous records, National Archives releases, and scholarly analyses.

## Background and Origins

The proposals emerged amid intense Cold War tensions following Fidel Castro's 1959 revolution in Cuba. Castro's government nationalized U.S. businesses, aligned with the Soviet Union, and allowed communist influence, which the U.S. viewed as a direct threat in its backyard. The failed Bay of Pigs invasion (April 1961) had humiliated the Kennedy administration and strengthened Castro's position. In response, the U.S. launched Operation Mongoose, a multi-agency covert program aimed at undermining and ultimately removing Castro through sabotage, propaganda, and support for Cuban exiles. As part of Mongoose planning, the JCS were asked in early 1962 for "pretexts" that could justify overt U.S. military intervention. The resulting Northwoods memorandum (dated March 13, 1962) was a collection of draft ideas compiled under the JCS to provide such justifications. It was framed as a preliminary planning document assuming that similar inputs from other agencies would help develop a time-phased invasion plan.

The core logic was that legitimate provocation was needed to overcome domestic and international opposition to another direct invasion attempt. The JCS recommended assigning both covert and overt aspects of any resulting operation to the military.

# Operation Northwoods Key Facts (Declassified JCS Memo)

- Date of main memorandum: March 13, 1962
- Presented to: Secretary of Defense Robert McNamara
- Authored/approved by: Joint Chiefs of Staff, chaired by Gen. Lyman L. Lemnitzer
- Context: Part of Operation Mongoose anti-Castro program
- Goal: Create plausible pretexts for U.S. military intervention in Cuba
- Status: Proposed only; never implemented; rejected by President Kennedy

The proposals were never executed, and no evidence indicates they advanced beyond the planning stage presented to McNamara.

## Specific Proposed False Flag Operations

The memorandum outlined numerous detailed scenarios, grouped under pretexts for intervention. Key examples include:

- **Attacks on U.S. Targets Blamed on Cuba**:
  - Blow up a U.S. ship in Guantanamo Bay and blame Cuba; create casualty lists in newspapers to generate "national indignation."
  - Stage a "Remember the Maine" incident by sinking a U.S. ship near the harbor entrance and conducting funerals for mock victims.
  - Simulate attacks on U.S. aircraft or Cuban actions in Latin America.

- **Aircraft Hijackings and Drone Scenarios**:
  - Hijack a U.S. civilian airliner or use a drone aircraft painted and registered to look like a civilian flight; stage its destruction (possibly with a real explosion after passengers are swapped to a military plane) and blame Cuban fighters.
  - Fake the shooting down of a chartered civil airliner en route from the U.S. to Jamaica, Guatemala, Panama, or Venezuela.

- **Terrorism and Sabotage in U.S. Cities and Against Exiles**:
  - Orchestrate terrorist acts in Miami, other Florida cities, or even Washington, D.C., including bombings and attacks on Cuban émigrés, then blame Castro agents.
  - Sink a boatload of Cuban refugees on the high seas and blame Cuban patrol boats.
  - Stage riots near the Guantanamo base or have "friendly" Cubans in uniform attack the base to simulate Cuban aggression.

- **Other Pretexts**:
  - Destroy U.S. military aircraft on the ground or in flight and fabricate evidence pointing to Cuba.
  - Use "friendly" Cubans to stage internal incidents that appear as Cuban government actions.

The plans emphasized creating "plausible" evidence, such as fabricated radio transmissions or discovered documents, to support the narrative of Cuban responsibility. The JCS stressed that these should appear as legitimate provocations.

**Trust note on proposals**: All listed scenarios appear explicitly in the declassified JCS memorandum (available via the National Security Archive PDF). They were presented as conceptual options for planning purposes, not as approved actions.

## Key Figures and Decision Process

- **Joint Chiefs of Staff**: Led by Chairman General Lyman L. Lemnitzer; the memo carried the collective weight of the JCS.
- **Robert McNamara**: Secretary of Defense; the document was sent to him for review.
- **President John F. Kennedy**: Ultimately rejected the proposals. Meeting notes from March 16, 1962, indicate Kennedy bluntly stated the U.S. was not discussing the use of military force at that time and highlighted other global commitments (e.g., Berlin).
- **Edward Lansdale**: Chief of operations for Mongoose; had requested the pretexts from the JCS.

Following the presentation, Lemnitzer was not immediately removed but was later reassigned as Supreme Allied Commander of NATO in 1963. No implementation occurred.

## Rejection and Aftermath

President Kennedy rejected the Northwoods proposals. While he continued to support covert actions under Mongoose, he drew the line at overt false flag operations that risked American lives and international credibility on such a scale. The exact reasoning is not fully documented in a single memo, but contemporary records show Kennedy's reluctance for direct military confrontation with Cuba at that moment due to broader geopolitical risks (including potential Soviet escalation). The plans remained classified for decades.

No criminal or disciplinary actions were taken against the JCS for proposing the ideas; they were viewed as contingency planning within the heated anti-Castro environment.

## Declassification and Public Revelation

The documents were declassified in stages:
- Portions released in 1997–1998 by the Assassination Records Review Board (linked to JFK records).
- More complete versions published online by the National Security Archive in 2001, coinciding with James Bamford's *Body of Secrets*.
- ABC News reported on the plans in 2001, bringing widespread public attention.

The full memorandum and related JCS papers are now available in the National Archives.

**Trust note**: Declassification timeline and content are confirmed by the National Security Archive, National Archives records, and contemporaneous news reports. The proposals are not disputed as authentic JCS documents.

## Combining Elements: False Flag Planning and Cold War Paranoia

Operation Northwoods combines high-level military planning, detailed deception tactics, and willingness to risk American lives for strategic gain:

> ## Overlapping Issues
>
> - Cold War desperation to remove Castro after Bay of Pigs failure.
> - Proposal of domestic and international false flag actions (attacks on U.S. soil or assets) to manufacture consent for war.
> - Involvement of the highest U.S. military leadership in drafting scenarios involving potential civilian casualties.
> - Rejection by civilian leadership (Kennedy), highlighting tension between military planners and elected officials.
> - Long-term secrecy until declassification decades later, fueling ongoing debates about government transparency and contingency planning.

These elements illustrate how far some officials were willing to go in the name of national security during the early 1960s.

## Conclusion

Operation Northwoods was a set of proposed false flag operations drafted by the U.S. Joint Chiefs of Staff in 1962 to justify military intervention in Cuba by staging or simulating attacks that could be blamed on the Castro regime. The plans included dramatic and potentially deadly scenarios such as blowing up U.S. ships, hijacking or destroying aircraft with drones, orchestrating terrorism in American cities, and sinking refugee boats. Presented to Secretary of Defense Robert McNamara as part of Operation Mongoose planning, the proposals were ultimately rejected by President John F. Kennedy, who was unwilling to pursue overt military force through such manufactured pretexts at the time. The documents, declassified in the late 1990s and early 2000s, provide a rare window into high-level contingency thinking during the Cold War. While never executed, Northwoods remains a documented example of how senior military leaders contemplated extreme deception tactics. For personal study, it highlights the thin line between aggressive planning and ethical boundaries in intelligence and military operations, as well as the importance of civilian oversight. It does not prove broader conspiracies but stands as verified evidence of proposed false flag concepts at the highest levels of the U.S. government.
